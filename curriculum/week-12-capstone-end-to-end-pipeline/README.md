# Week 12 — Capstone: End-to-End Reproducible Bioinformatics Pipeline

> **Educational and research use only.** This week is the C10 capstone. You will design, build, document, and ship an end-to-end bioinformatics pipeline on a real public dataset of your choosing, using a workflow manager (Snakemake or Nextflow; both free) and reproducible software environments (Conda + bioconda free; Singularity or Docker for tool isolation). **Nothing produced in this week is clinical software.** No variant call, no expression difference, no phylogenetic tree, no assembled contig in this capstone may be used to guide patient care, public-health policy, agricultural deployment, or regulatory decisions. The point of the capstone is not the answer; the point is the *pipeline* — a piece of work that another person on another laptop in another year can re-run, byte-identical, from the deposited data plus the deposited code plus the deposited run instructions. Pipelines that produce the right answer for the wrong reasons (a hard-coded path, an un-pinned tool version, a Conda environment captured at coffee-break time but not at commit time) fail this bar. Pipelines that produce a slightly different answer because of a clearly-named non-determinism (a `--seed` flag set to the current time, a multi-threaded reduction that is not associative) but document the source of the variation pass it. The capstone's pass criterion is reproducibility, not correctness.

> **Reproducibility note.** Reproducibility in bioinformatics has been studied empirically. The 2009 Ioannidis et al. review in *Nature Genetics* (10:843; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2778103/>) attempted to reproduce 18 published microarray analyses and reproduced two of them. The 2018 Beaulieu-Jones and Greene preprint estimated that 26% of computational biology papers were reproducible from the data and code alone. The 2020 Mangul et al. survey in *Genome Biology* (21:22; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6995158/>) found that 26 of 50 surveyed bioinformatics tools could not be installed from the published instructions without modification. None of these failure rates is acceptable for an undergraduate's published capstone, and most of the failures are fixed by three things: a pinned software environment (Conda + bioconda, captured to `environment.yml` at commit time), a pipeline-as-code workflow manager (Snakemake or Nextflow, not a shell script), and a container (Singularity or Docker) wrapping the whole thing for systems where Conda cannot bootstrap. The capstone requires all three.

In Weeks 1-11 of C10 you took fragments of the standard bioinformatics stack and used them: FASTA / FASTQ parsing in Week 2; pairwise sequence alignment (Needleman-Wunsch, Smith-Waterman) in Week 3; BLAST and taxonomic placement in Week 4; short-read alignment (BWA, minimap2) in Week 5; germline variant calling (GATK HaplotypeCaller, bcftools call) in Week 6; bulk RNA-seq differential expression (Salmon + tximport + DESeq2) in Week 7; variant annotation and clinical interpretation (snpEff, VEP, ClinVar) in Week 8; multiple sequence alignment and phylogenetics (MAFFT, IQ-TREE) in Week 9; long-read sequencing and de novo assembly (badread, Flye, Medaka, BUSCO) in Week 10; and somatic variant calling with mutational signatures (Mutect2, FilterMutectCalls, Strelka2, SigProfilerAssignment, OncoKB / CIViC look-up) in Week 11. Each of those weeks was a single technique on a small didactic dataset, and the mini-project closed with a CLI script and a `run-info.json` that pinned every parameter.

Week 12 is different. The point of the capstone is not to learn another technique; it is to compose the techniques you already know into a piece of work that a *reviewer* could run. The deliverable is not a Jupyter notebook with the answer in it; the deliverable is a GitHub repository (or a Zenodo-deposited archive; free DOI service for academic uploads at <https://zenodo.org/>) that contains the code, the `Snakefile` or `main.nf`, the `environment.yml`, the container recipe (`Singularity.def` or `Dockerfile`), a `README.md` with the run command, a `run-info.json` capturing the run that produced the figures, and the figures and tables themselves. The pipeline can be small (a single SNV in a single sample) or medium (a small public dataset; 10-50 samples). It does not need to be a publication. It needs to be re-runnable.

You will pick one of four research-flavored project tracks: (1) **variant discovery** on a public Genome in a Bottle (GIAB) sample (HG002 chr20 subset; free at <https://www.nist.gov/programs-projects/genome-bottle>) — align FASTQ, call variants, compare against the GIAB truth set, report precision / recall / F1; (2) **RNA-seq differential expression** on a small GEO study (a published series with 6-20 samples, two conditions; free metadata + FASTQ at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/>) — quantify with Salmon, test with DESeq2, produce a volcano plot and a top-gene table; (3) **MSA and phylogeny of a viral family** (e.g. a 30-100-genome SARS-CoV-2 or influenza-A subset from NCBI Virus, free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/labs/virus/>) — align with MAFFT, build a tree with IQ-TREE, visualize the tree, annotate the tips by sampling date and country; or (4) **long-read assembly of a small bacterial genome** (a 4-5 Mb species; reads from SRA, free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/sra>) — assemble with Flye, polish with Medaka, quality-check with BUSCO and Quast, output a polished FASTA with a `run-info.json`. All four tracks have been demonstrated in Weeks 6, 7, 9, 10 respectively at the technique level; the capstone scales them to a real public sample and wraps them as a pipeline.

The pipeline is the work. The number of samples and the depth of analysis are secondary. A 5-sample RNA-seq study with a clean Snakemake DAG, pinned tool versions, a Conda lockfile, a Singularity image that bootstraps from `bootstrap: docker` on a clean machine, and a `README.md` that runs end-to-end with one `snakemake --cores 4 --use-conda` invocation, is a passing capstone. A 200-sample analysis with bespoke shell scripts, no version pinning, no environment file, and a `README.md` that says "run the scripts in order" is not a passing capstone even if the analysis is more ambitious. The capstone is graded on the artefact's *reproducibility profile*, not on the analytic depth.

The second half of the week is the **wrap-up sidebar**: a one-paragraph retrospective on each of Weeks 1-12. You write a paragraph naming what each week taught, what stuck, and what you would do differently. The retrospective lives in `wrap-up.md` next to your capstone repo's `README.md` and travels with the deposited archive. It is part of the grade.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Choose** a research-flavored question that uses techniques covered in Weeks 1-11 and is reproducible on a free public dataset within the time budget of a single-person capstone (estimate: 25-40 hours of work over five days; total dataset under 50 GB; total runtime under 6 hours on a 16 GB laptop or a free academic cluster allocation).
- **Design** a Snakemake (Mölder et al. 2021, *F1000Research* 10:33; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8114187/>) or Nextflow (Di Tommaso et al. 2017, *Nature Biotechnology* 35:316; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8224876/>) workflow that expresses the pipeline as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of rules / processes, with each rule's `input`, `output`, `params`, `threads`, `resources`, and `conda` declared explicitly. Render the DAG with `snakemake --dag | dot -Tsvg > dag.svg` or `nextflow run main.nf -with-dag dag.svg` and commit the SVG to the repo. The DAG image is one of the capstone's graded artefacts.
- **Pin** every tool version with a Conda environment file. Use bioconda (Grüning et al. 2018, *Nature Methods* 15:475; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11070151/>) and conda-forge as the channels. Capture the resolved environment with `conda env export --from-history > environment.yml` for the human-readable view and `conda list --explicit > environment.lock.txt` for the byte-identical reproduction view. Commit both. Re-creating the environment on a new machine should be one `conda env create -f environment.yml` call away.
- **Containerize** the pipeline with Singularity (Kurtzer et al. 2017, *PLoS ONE* 12:e0177459; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5426675/>; now maintained as Apptainer) or Docker. Use the workflow manager's container integration: Snakemake's `singularity:` directive at the rule level or the global `--use-singularity --singularity-args` flag; Nextflow's `container` directive in `nextflow.config`. Build the container from a `Singularity.def` or `Dockerfile` that captures the same Conda environment plus the workflow manager itself. The container goal is *bootstrapping reproducibility*: a clean machine with Singularity / Apptainer installed should be able to re-run the pipeline with the published `.sif` image alone.
- **Capture** the deposited-data + run-instructions trio: (a) the input data deposited (or, if too large, the accession URLs and a `download.sh` script with checksums); (b) the code (the Git repo with a tag matching the run, e.g. `v1.0`); (c) the run instructions (the `README.md` with the exact one-line command that produced the figures, plus an `environment.yml`, an `environment.lock.txt`, a `Singularity.def`, and a `run-info.json`).
- **Deposit** the capstone artefact on Zenodo (free DOI for academic upload at <https://zenodo.org/>) or as a tagged Git release on GitHub. Embed the DOI in the `README.md` and the `CITATION.cff`. The capstone gets a DOI; it is a citeable piece of work.
- **Write** a one-page report (`report.md`) describing the question, the dataset, the pipeline, the results, and the limitations. The limitations section names which Weeks 1-12 caveats apply: variant calls without orthogonal validation are not clinical (Weeks 6, 8, 11); RNA-seq DE on small n is statistically thin (Week 7); a phylogenetic tree with low bootstrap support tells you the data does not constrain the topology (Week 9); a draft genome assembly is not a finished genome (Week 10); a pipeline run by one person on one dataset has not been reproduced (Week 12).
- **Write** the wrap-up sidebar: a paragraph per week (W1-W12) covering what the week taught, what you remember, and what you would do differently if you were the curriculum author. The sidebar is the C10-end retrospective. Save as `wrap-up.md` next to the capstone `README.md`.
- **Demonstrate** that the pipeline is reproducible by re-running it on a clean machine (a freshly-spun-up Codespace, a clean Conda environment, a clean Singularity bind) and confirming byte-identical output for the deterministic rules and within-tolerance output (cosine similarity > 0.99 on the result figures' underlying TSVs, BLEU-style hash agreement on the variant VCFs) for the stochastic rules.
- **Reason about** the standard reproducibility failure modes: un-pinned tool versions (the canonical mistake; `samtools` without a version constraint resolves to whatever the channel ships today); un-pinned reference data (a "GRCh38" alignment against a regenerated FASTA can have one-base coordinate drift if the reference includes / excludes the EBV decoy); un-pinned random seeds (Flye, IQ-TREE, DESeq2's `set.seed`); un-pinned thread counts (multi-threaded reductions are non-associative; a 4-thread run produces a slightly different VCF from a 16-thread run for some callers); un-pinned filesystem ordering (`os.listdir` is order-dependent across filesystems; sort everything before iterating).

## Topics covered

- **The reproducibility crisis in computational biology.** The empirical results of Ioannidis et al. 2009, Beaulieu-Jones and Greene 2018, and Mangul et al. 2020 frame the field's reproducibility baseline. The capstone is the response: a deposited code-plus-data-plus-instructions artefact that another person can re-run.
- **Pipeline-as-code: Snakemake and Nextflow.** Both express the workflow as a DAG of computational rules. Snakemake (Mölder et al. 2021) is Python-syntax, file-name-pattern-driven, and has tight Conda / Singularity integration. Nextflow (Di Tommaso et al. 2017) is Groovy-syntax, channel-driven, and has tight Docker / Singularity / Kubernetes integration. Both are free, both have large user bases, both are appropriate for a capstone. We teach Snakemake as the default and show the equivalent Nextflow for one rule per lecture.
- **Conda and bioconda.** Conda is the cross-platform package manager; bioconda (Grüning et al. 2018) is the bioinformatics channel with 8000+ packages. The standard environment specification is the `environment.yml`. The reproducibility-grade specification is the `--explicit` lockfile (`conda list --explicit > environment.lock.txt`), which pins every package's URL and SHA-256 hash. Capstone-grade work commits both.
- **Containers: Singularity / Apptainer and Docker.** Singularity (Kurtzer et al. 2017; renamed to Apptainer post-fork) is the HPC-friendly container runtime: rootless execution, single-file `.sif` image, no daemon. Docker is the developer-friendly container runtime: daemonized, layered images, ubiquitous on cloud. Both are free for open-source projects. The capstone uses Singularity / Apptainer for the deposited artefact because `.sif` files are single-file deposit-friendly objects.
- **The deposited-data + run-instructions trio.** The three deliverables of a reproducible pipeline: the data (or accession URLs), the code (the Git repo with a tag), and the instructions (the `README.md` with the one-line run command, plus `environment.yml`, `environment.lock.txt`, `Singularity.def`, `run-info.json`). Without all three, reproduction fails.
- **DOI deposits with Zenodo.** Zenodo (<https://zenodo.org/>) is the CERN-hosted free data repository for academic deposits. A GitHub release tagged `v1.0` can be archived to Zenodo with one click via the GitHub-Zenodo integration; the resulting DOI is permanent and citeable.
- **Run-info.json: the inline provenance record.** The pattern from Weeks 8-11. Every pipeline run writes a `run-info.json` containing the workflow manager version, the Conda environment name, the Singularity image SHA, the input data accessions, the random seeds, the host name, the user, the date, the Git commit SHA, and any non-default parameters. The `run-info.json` travels with the output and is part of the deposit.
- **Project track 1: variant discovery.** Align FASTQ to GRCh38 chr20 (subset from a Genome in a Bottle sample, free at NIST); call variants with DeepVariant or GATK HaplotypeCaller; compare against the GIAB truth VCF with `hap.py` (free at <https://github.com/Illumina/hap.py>); report precision, recall, F1, and stratified per-region metrics.
- **Project track 2: RNA-seq differential expression.** Download a 6-20-sample GEO series (e.g. GSE52778: airway smooth muscle cells +/- dexamethasone; 8 samples; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE52778>); quantify with Salmon against GENCODE v44; aggregate transcripts to genes with tximport; test with DESeq2; produce a volcano plot and a top-30-gene table.
- **Project track 3: MSA + phylogeny.** Download 30-100 viral genomes from NCBI Virus (e.g. SARS-CoV-2 lineage B.1.617.2 from a single country, single month); align with MAFFT --auto; trim alignment with trimAl; build tree with IQ-TREE 2 under model `GTR+G+I` with 1000 ultrafast bootstraps; visualize with ggtree or ete3; tip-annotate by date and country.
- **Project track 4: long-read assembly.** Download Nanopore reads for a small bacterial genome from SRA (e.g. *E. coli* K-12 MG1655 R10.4 reads, 50-100x coverage); assemble with Flye; polish with Medaka; quality-check with BUSCO (bacteria_odb10) and Quast; output a polished FASTA with a `run-info.json` capturing every tool's version and seed.
- **DAG rendering.** Every workflow manager can render its DAG. Snakemake: `snakemake --dag | dot -Tsvg > dag.svg`. Nextflow: `nextflow run main.nf -with-dag dag.svg`. The DAG visualizes the rule graph and is the canonical artefact that shows the workflow's structure at a glance. Commit it.
- **The capstone retrospective.** A wrap-up `wrap-up.md` with one paragraph per Week 1-12. What the week taught, what stuck, what you would do differently. The retrospective is the C10-end reflection and lives next to the capstone repo's `README.md`.
- **Standard reproducibility failure modes.** Un-pinned versions (the canonical mistake). Un-pinned reference data. Un-pinned random seeds. Un-pinned thread counts (some reductions are non-associative). Un-pinned filesystem order. Hidden network calls (a tool that auto-downloads a database the first time it runs is not reproducible offline). Time-dependent output (a tool that embeds the current timestamp in the output VCF header is not byte-identical between runs). The capstone write-up names which of these its pipeline could plausibly suffer from.

## Wrap-up sidebar: C10 in retrospect

C10 ran 12 weeks. Here is the path the curriculum walked, week by week, with the through-line. The student's own retrospective lives in `wrap-up.md`; this section is the *curriculum's* retrospective.

- **Week 1 — Vocabulary and ethics.** Sequence, read, contig, assembly, alignment, variant, reference, build, annotation, expression, phylogeny. The IRB story for human subjects research, the dbGaP authorized-access model, the de-identification standard, the why-we-do-not-publish-raw-FASTQ-of-identifiable-humans rule. The week that made the rest of C10 possible to discuss responsibly.
- **Week 2 — FASTA / FASTQ I / O.** The two text formats that hold every base-level dataset in the field. Phred quality encoding, the Sanger-vs-Illumina-vs-Solexa offset history, the gzip-vs-bgzip distinction, the streaming-iterator pattern, Biopython's SeqIO. The week that put a parser in your toolbox.
- **Week 3 — Pairwise alignment.** Needleman-Wunsch (global) and Smith-Waterman (local). Dynamic programming. Scoring matrices (BLOSUM62, PAM250). Gap-open and gap-extend penalties. The week that explained why every later tool's `--gap-open 10 --gap-extend 1` line is not arbitrary.
- **Week 4 — BLAST and taxonomy.** Heuristic local alignment at scale, the BLAST E-value, the seed-and-extend algorithm, the NCBI tax-IDs, Kraken2 / Bracken for metagenomics taxonomy. The week that explained why a sequence search returns a *ranked list*, not an answer.
- **Week 5 — Read alignment.** BWA-MEM for short reads, minimap2 for long and short, the SAM / BAM format, samtools, the MAPQ score, the duplicate-marking step. The week that produced the BAM, the input to every downstream caller.
- **Week 6 — Variant calling.** GATK HaplotypeCaller and bcftools call for germline SNVs and indels. The VCF format, the genotype likelihoods, the joint-genotyping idea, the filter tiers (hard-filter vs VQSR). The week that produced the germline VCF.
- **Week 7 — Transcriptomics and RNA-seq.** Salmon for pseudoalignment / quasi-mapping, tximport for transcript-to-gene aggregation, DESeq2 for the negative-binomial differential-expression test, the volcano plot. The week that moved from DNA to RNA.
- **Week 8 — Variant annotation and interpretation.** snpEff and VEP for functional annotation, ClinVar for clinical-significance lookup, the ACMG / AMP variant-classification framework at the concept level. The week that explained why a variant call is the start, not the end.
- **Week 9 — Phylogenetics and MSA.** MAFFT and Clustal Omega for multiple sequence alignment, IQ-TREE for maximum-likelihood phylogeny, the bootstrap, the substitution-model selection (ModelTest-NG, IQ-TREE's built-in ModelFinder). The week that explained how a tree is inferred and how confident the inference is.
- **Week 10 — Long-read sequencing and assembly.** Nanopore and PacBio chemistry at the concept level, badread for simulation, Flye for OLC assembly, Medaka for polishing, BUSCO and Quast for assembly QC. The week that produced a genome.
- **Week 11 — Cancer genomics.** Mutect2 + FilterMutectCalls for matched-pair somatic variant calling, Strelka2 as a cross-check, GATK CalculateContamination for purity, SigProfilerAssignment for COSMIC v3 signature decomposition, OncoKB / CIViC for clinical-interpretation lookup. The week that put the patient-care guardrails on every variant call.
- **Week 12 — Capstone.** Snakemake or Nextflow for pipeline-as-code, Conda + bioconda for environment pinning, Singularity / Apptainer for the container, Zenodo for the DOI. The week that turned every prior week's CLI script into a deposited, citeable pipeline.

## A note on the data size

The four project tracks are deliberately sized to fit a 16 GB laptop:

- **Variant discovery:** HG002 chr20 subset BAM (~2 GB) + GRCh38 chr20 reference (~63 MB) + GIAB truth VCF for chr20 (~50 MB). Total ~2.2 GB. Runtime: 30-90 minutes for align + call + hap.py.
- **RNA-seq DE:** GSE52778 (8 samples; ~30 GB of FASTQ; trim to chr1+chr19+chr21 for a teaching subset, ~5 GB) + GENCODE v44 chr1+chr19+chr21 transcriptome (~80 MB). Total ~5 GB. Runtime: 1-3 hours for Salmon + tximport + DESeq2.
- **MSA + phylogeny:** 50-genome SARS-CoV-2 subset (~1.5 MB of FASTA) + MAFFT + IQ-TREE. Total well under 100 MB. Runtime: 5-20 minutes.
- **Long-read assembly:** *E. coli* K-12 MG1655 R10.4 reads at 60x (~3 GB of FASTQ) + Flye + Medaka + BUSCO. Total ~3 GB. Runtime: 45-90 minutes on 8 cores.

Commit small input subsets (under 100 MB total per repo) and use a `download.sh` script with checksums for anything larger. ## Stretch goals

If you finish early and want to push further, try any of the following:

- Read the Singularity paper (Kurtzer et al. 2017) at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5426675/>. The single-file SIF format and rootless execution model explain why HPC sites prefer Singularity over Docker.
