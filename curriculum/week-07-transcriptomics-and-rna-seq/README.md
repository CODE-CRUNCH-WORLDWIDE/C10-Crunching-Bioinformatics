# Week 7 — Transcriptomics and RNA-seq

In Week 6 you turned a sorted, duplicate-marked BAM into a filtered, VEP-annotated VCF: 168 SNPs and 22 indels on 4.6 Mb of *E. coli* MG1655, hard-filtered to 152 SNPs (90.5%) and 19 indels (86.4%) PASS, Ts/Tv 0.94, 57.2% of PASS SNPs missense. That was the DNA-sequencing closer of the arc. Week 7 leaves DNA behind. The reads are still 100-150 bp short Illumina sequences, the FASTQ format is unchanged, the QC tools are unchanged — but the molecules behind the reads are now **mRNA fragments**, the reference is no longer a chromosome but a **transcriptome**, and the output of the week is no longer a variant call but an **expression-counts matrix**: one row per gene (or transcript), one column per sample, integer or estimated-fractional counts in each cell. RNA-seq is the workhorse of modern functional genomics. Every cancer-genomics paper, every plant-stress paper, every microbial-physiology paper that wants to say "gene X went up" or "pathway Y was activated" is built on top of a counts matrix that looks exactly like the one you will produce by Friday. The canonical tools this week are **`fastp`** (Chen et al. 2018, *Bioinformatics* 34:i884) for adapter trimming and per-read QC, **`kallisto`** (Bray et al. 2016, *Nature Biotechnology* 34:525) and **`Salmon`** (Patro et al. 2017, *Nature Methods* 14:417) for transcript-level pseudoalignment and quantification, **`HISAT2`** (Kim et al. 2019, *Nature Biotechnology* 37:907) and **`STAR`** (Dobin et al. 2013, *Bioinformatics* 29:15) for splice-aware genomic alignment when you need a BAM, and **`featureCounts`** from the `subread` suite (Liao et al. 2014, *Bioinformatics* 30:923) for the alignment-based counting step. All five are free, all five are documented, and all five are the tools you will be running on real data from Monday onward.

By Friday of Week 7 you will be able to take a raw paired-end RNA-seq FASTQ pair, run `fastp` to trim adapters and low-quality 3' tails and produce a QC report, build a `kallisto` index against a transcriptome FASTA (e.g. GENCODE basic for human, Ensembl for any other species), run `kallisto quant` to produce per-transcript estimated counts and TPM in under a minute per sample on a laptop, run `salmon quant` (the Patro et al. 2017 alternative) on the same FASTQs and confirm that the two pseudoaligners agree to within ~2% per gene on well-expressed transcripts, run `HISAT2` + `samtools sort` + `featureCounts -a annotation.gtf -t exon -g gene_id` as the classical alignment-based comparison and recover essentially the same gene-level counts as the pseudoalignment path, compute **TPM** (transcripts per million; Wagner et al. 2012) and **CPM** (counts per million) from a raw counts table by hand in Python, and assemble a three-sample counts matrix (one row per gene, three columns) ready for a PCA and a downstream differential-expression analysis in Week 8. The mini-project assembles such a matrix from a no-cost SRA toy dataset (three samples of *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* under glucose vs galactose growth, from the Bioconductor `airway`-style teaching subsets), runs PCA on the log-CPM-normalized matrix, and writes up a one-page biological interpretation: "PC1 separates carbon source, PC2 separates replicate batch; the top loadings on PC1 include `GAL1`, `GAL7`, `GAL10` — the canonical galactose-utilization operon."

The other half of the week is **how RNA-seq is different from DNA-seq, in ways that matter**. A DNA-seq read at coverage 30x at chromosome 1, position 150,123 is one of 30 independent observations of that base in the diploid genome. An RNA-seq read at gene `GAPDH` is one of *millions* of observations of `GAPDH` mRNA in a cell — RNA-seq is not coverage of a static template, it is *sampling* of a transcript abundance distribution. This changes everything downstream. It changes the alignment strategy (a read spanning an exon-exon junction in a multi-exon mRNA cannot align as a single contiguous block to the genome the way a DNA read can, so the aligner must be *splice-aware*; this is why STAR and HISAT2 exist and bwa mem does not work for RNA on a vertebrate genome). It changes the quantification strategy (a read that came from an mRNA of `MYC` might also have come from `MYCL1` and `MYCN` because the three are paralogues with 80% sequence identity, so the quantifier must do *probabilistic assignment* of multi-mapping reads, not just pick one; this is the **Expectation-Maximization** step at the heart of kallisto, Salmon, and RSEM). It changes the normalization strategy (you cannot compare raw counts across samples because the total read count varies by library, and you cannot compare raw counts across genes because long genes intrinsically generate more reads per molecule of mRNA; this is why TPM, FPKM, and CPM all exist, and why the literature is full of arguments about which one to use in which situation — Pachter 2011, *arXiv*:1104.3889; Wagner et al. 2012, *Theory in Biosciences* 131:281). The canonical answer for cross-sample comparison is "use raw counts going into a differential-expression tool (`DESeq2`, `edgeR`, `limma-voom`); use TPM going into a heatmap, a PCA, or any human-eye comparison." We come back to this distinction in Week 8.

The third theme is **alignment vs pseudoalignment**, and why two architecturally different approaches both produce roughly the same counts matrix. **Alignment** (the classical path, STAR/HISAT2 + featureCounts) does what Week 5 did, with a splice-aware twist: place each read at a single best position in the genome, output a BAM, and have a counting tool walk the BAM and tally reads overlapping each gene annotation in a GTF. **Pseudoalignment** (the new path, kallisto/Salmon, Bray et al. 2016) does something deceptively different: never align a read to anything, instead use a k-mer index to find the *set of transcripts compatible with each read* in `O(read_length)` time, then run EM to estimate the relative abundance of each transcript consistent with all the read-to-transcript-set assignments. Pseudoalignment is **100x faster** (kallisto runs a full human RNA-seq sample in ~3 minutes on a laptop, where STAR takes 30-60 minutes), recovers nearly identical gene-level counts (~99% concordance on well-behaved samples; Patro et al. 2017, Soneson et al. 2015), and is the right tool for any project that does not specifically need a BAM (e.g. for SNP calling on transcripts or for novel-isoform discovery — those still need a real alignment). Memorize the trade-off: **alignment when you need a BAM, pseudoalignment when you need a counts matrix.** Most modern RNA-seq pipelines pseudoalign.

## Topics covered

- **The RNA-seq experimental workflow**: RNA extraction from cells or tissue → optional ribosomal-RNA depletion or polyA selection → reverse transcription to cDNA → fragmentation → adapter ligation → PCR amplification → Illumina paired-end sequencing → FASTQ output. Each step has a characteristic QC signature in the resulting reads. Reference: Conesa et al. 2016, "A survey of best practices for RNA-seq data analysis," *Genome Biology* 17:13 — the open-access "Hitchhiker's Guide" that every RNA-seq tutorial cites.
- **The transcriptome vs the genome**: a genome FASTA has one record per chromosome (~25 records, ~3 GB for human GRCh38). A transcriptome FASTA has one record per transcript (~200,000 records, ~300 MB for human GENCODE basic). The transcriptome is **smaller** than the genome because it skips introns, intergenic regions, and the centromere. The transcriptome is the natural reference for RNA-seq quantification: a read came from a transcript, so the right thing to do is map it to a transcript.
- **GENCODE and Ensembl as transcript-annotation sources**: GENCODE is the human/mouse reference annotation maintained jointly by EBI/Sanger/UCSC (<https://www.gencodegenes.org/>). Ensembl is the cross-species equivalent (<https://www.ensembl.org/>). Both publish FASTA transcript sequences and GFF3/GTF annotation files. The "basic" GENCODE annotation contains ~100,000 transcripts of ~60,000 genes; the comprehensive set adds another ~150,000 transcripts including TEC and putative pseudogenes.
- **The GFF3/GTF specification**: tab-separated, nine columns (`seqid`, `source`, `type`, `start`, `end`, `score`, `strand`, `phase`, `attributes`). `type` is one of `gene`, `transcript`, `exon`, `CDS`, `five_prime_UTR`, `three_prime_UTR`. `attributes` is a semicolon-separated `key=value` list including `gene_id`, `transcript_id`, `gene_name`. GFF3 spec: <http://gmod.org/wiki/GFF3>. GTF is the older variant with `key "value"` instead of `key=value`. Most tools accept both; some are picky.
- **Adapter trimming with `fastp`**: paired-end Illumina libraries have a known adapter sequence at the 3' end of each read; if the insert was shorter than the read length, the adapter is sequenced and shows up in the data. `fastp` auto-detects the adapter and trims it. `fastp` also trims low-quality 3' tails (default: Q20), trims polyG tails (NovaSeq artifact), reports per-base quality, GC content, duplication estimate, and adapter content. Output: trimmed FASTQs + HTML/JSON QC report. Reference: Chen et al. 2018.
- **The pseudoalignment idea (Bray et al. 2016)**: build a colored de Bruijn graph of k-mers from the transcriptome, k=31 by default. Each k-mer is a node; each transcript is a path; each k-mer is labelled with the set of transcripts it appears in (the **k-mer's color set**). To "pseudoalign" a read, look up each of its k-mers in the index and intersect the color sets. The result is the **compatibility class** of the read: the set of transcripts the read could have come from. The read is never aligned base-by-base. The compatibility class plus the read counts goes into an EM algorithm that estimates per-transcript abundance. Total runtime: ~3 minutes per human RNA-seq sample on a laptop. Reference: Bray et al. 2016.
- **Salmon's refinement (Patro et al. 2017)**: kallisto's pure pseudoalignment can be tripped up by reads that hit the k-mer index but do not actually align well at the base level (e.g. reads with sequencing errors, reads from sequence variants). Salmon adds **selective alignment**: after pseudoalignment identifies a candidate transcript set, do a quick Smith-Waterman-like alignment score check against each candidate, and downweight or reject transcripts where the actual alignment score is poor. This recovers ~1-2% accuracy at the cost of ~2x runtime. Salmon also implements a richer fragment-bias model and a richer GC-bias correction. Reference: Patro et al. 2017.
- **The EM algorithm in 1-2 paragraphs**: given a set of reads each with a compatibility class (set of transcripts), and an unknown vector `θ` of per-transcript abundances, alternate between (E-step) computing the expected fractional assignment of each read to each transcript in its compatibility class given the current `θ`, and (M-step) updating `θ` as the normalized sum of fractional assignments. Converges in 50-200 iterations. The output is per-transcript estimated counts and TPM. Both kallisto and Salmon implement this; the implementations differ in initialization, optimization (kallisto uses standard EM, Salmon uses a variational Bayesian inference variant), and bias modeling.
- **The classical alignment path**: `HISAT2` (Kim et al. 2019) and `STAR` (Dobin et al. 2013) are **splice-aware** short-read aligners. They handle reads that span exon-exon junctions by finding the longer aligned segments on the genome and inferring the splice. STAR builds a suffix-array index of the genome (~28 GB for human); HISAT2 builds an FM-index of the genome plus a "graph FM-index" of known splice junctions (~4 GB for human). Both output BAM. `featureCounts` (Liao et al. 2014) then walks the BAM, intersects each read with each annotated exon in the GTF, and tallies reads per gene (or per exon, or per transcript). Output: a TSV with one row per gene, one column per sample.
- **TPM, CPM, RPKM/FPKM**: the per-gene-and-per-sample normalization options. **CPM** (counts per million) corrects for library size only: `CPM = 10^6 · count / library_size`. **RPKM/FPKM** (Mortazavi et al. 2008, *Nature Methods* 5:621) corrects for library size and gene length: `RPKM = 10^9 · count / (library_size · gene_length_kb)`. **TPM** (Wagner et al. 2012) corrects for library size and gene length but normalizes after the per-gene scaling, so the per-sample sum is always 10^6: `TPM = 10^6 · (count / length) / sum_g(count_g / length_g)`. The TPM definition is preferred over RPKM because TPMs sum to a constant across samples, which makes cross-sample comparison meaningful. Reference: Pachter 2011 (the cleanest derivation), Wagner et al. 2012 (the original TPM paper).
- **Common failure modes**: **rRNA contamination** — if rRNA depletion failed, 50-90% of reads will map to the ~6 rRNA loci in the transcriptome, inflating effective library size and crushing per-gene counts elsewhere. Signal: `fastp` HTML shows very high duplication, kallisto `n_pseudoaligned` is high but per-non-rRNA-gene counts are low. **3' bias** — polyA-selection biases reads toward the 3' end of each transcript; `fastp`'s per-base coverage plot shows a steep drop at the 5' end. Mild bias is normal; severe bias (e.g. all reads in the last 200 bp) indicates RNA degradation. **Multi-mapping reads in paralog families** — paralogues with high sequence identity (~80%) generate reads that map to multiple transcripts; the EM step assigns fractional counts, which is correct but confuses downstream tools that expect integers. Standard signal: `kallisto` `est_counts` column has many non-integer values for paralogous genes.

## A note on the data size

RNA-seq files are bigger than DNA-seq files for the same yield of usable signal, because library complexity matters more (mRNA abundance spans 6 orders of magnitude) and many sequencing reads land on the same handful of highly expressed genes. The mini-project per-sample data is:

- **~150 MB** as paired-end gzipped FASTQs per yeast sample (3.2 M paired reads × 75 bp × 2).
- **~10 MB** as the kallisto `abundance.h5` + `abundance.tsv` per sample.
- **~250 MB** as the HISAT2-produced sorted BAM per sample (much bigger than the count matrix, because every read's alignment is preserved).
- **~50 KB** as the final 3-sample counts matrix TSV.

Commit the counts matrix and the per-sample `abundance.tsv` and the QC HTML; gitignore the FASTQs and the BAMs. For a human RNA-seq sample (30 M paired-end reads), multiply everything by ~10x.
