# Week 8 — Variant Annotation and Interpretation

> **Educational and research use only.** Nothing in this week's material is intended for clinical diagnosis, treatment selection, or any other clinical decision-making. Variant interpretation in a clinical context requires a CLIA-certified laboratory, board-certified clinical geneticists, and the full ACMG/AMP workflow with manual review. The pipelines you build this week are the same *mechanical* steps a clinical pipeline performs, but they are not those pipelines. Do not act on these outputs.

In Week 6 you took a sorted, duplicate-marked BAM and produced a filtered, hard-quality-controlled VCF: 168 SNPs and 22 indels on 4.6 Mb of *E. coli* MG1655, hard-filtered to 152 SNPs (90.5%) PASS and 19 indels (86.4%) PASS, Ts/Tv 0.94, 57.2% of PASS SNPs in missense codons. In Week 7 you parked DNA-seq and learned RNA-seq: a counts matrix, TPM normalization, kallisto pseudoalignment, the GAL regulon induced 500x on galactose. Week 8 returns to the variant calling thread. The input is no longer a FASTQ pair; it is a VCF. The output is no longer a counts matrix; it is a **per-variant interpretation report** that answers, for each row of the VCF, three questions: *what does this variant DO to the protein, how often is it seen in healthy populations, and is it known to be associated with a disease?*

The canonical tools for those three questions are **VEP** (Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor, McLaren et al. 2016, *Genome Biology* 17:122) and **SnpEff** (Cingolani et al. 2012, *Fly* 6:80) for the "what does it do to the protein" axis; **gnomAD** (Karczewski et al. 2020, *Nature* 581:434) for the "how often is it seen" axis; and **ClinVar** (Landrum et al. 2018, *Nucleic Acids Research* 46:D1062) plus **dbSNP** (Sherry et al. 2001, *Nucleic Acids Research* 29:308) for the "is it known to be associated with a disease" axis. Functional impact prediction adds two more named tools: **SIFT** (Ng and Henikoff 2003, *Nucleic Acids Research* 31:3812) for sequence-based deleteriousness and **PolyPhen-2** (Adzhubei et al. 2010, *Nature Methods* 7:248) for structural-context-based deleteriousness. Finally, **ACMG/AMP 2015** (Richards et al. 2015, *Genetics in Medicine* 17:405) is the canonical guideline for combining all of the above into a five-tier classification (Benign / Likely Benign / VUS / Likely Pathogenic / Pathogenic), and **PharmGKB** (Whirl-Carrillo et al. 2021, *Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics* 110:563) is the canonical free pharmacogenomics knowledge base for variants that affect drug response. Every tool and every database named above is free and publicly accessible.

By Friday of Week 8 you will be able to take a small demo VCF (we use a ~50-variant subset of the GIAB NA12878 truth set plus a few hand-picked ClinVar variants for didactic clarity), parse it in Python with **`cyvcf2`** (Pedersen and Quinlan 2017, *Bioinformatics* 33:1867), annotate each variant against a local copy of the Ensembl VEP cache or via SnpEff's `databases.zip`, query the per-variant ClinVar, dbSNP, and gnomAD records, compute SIFT and PolyPhen-2 scores via the VEP plugin output, apply the ACMG criteria (BA1, PVS1, PM2, PP3, BS1, etc.) programmatically where the criteria are mechanically applicable, drop the criteria that are not (most "P" criteria require manual case-level review), and emit a CSV or HTML report with one row per variant and columns for each axis. The mini-project assembles such a pipeline end to end on a fresh laptop: input a VCF, output a `variants.html` report that a non-bioinformatician colleague could read. The colleague will not see anything they can act on clinically. They will see a defensible, reproducible, version-pinned summary of what each variant is, where it sits in the genome, and what the public databases say about it.

The other half of the week is **how to be careful with this output**. Variant annotation is mechanical; variant *interpretation* is judgmental. VEP will tell you that `chr7:117559590 G>A` (a real coordinate in `CFTR`) is `missense_variant`, that it changes residue 508 from F to absent (the famous `delta-F508` CFTR mutation, the most common cystic fibrosis variant), that gnomAD shows this variant at an allele frequency of ~0.7% in non-Finnish Europeans, that ClinVar reports it as Pathogenic with a 4-star review status, and that SIFT predicts it deleterious with a score of 0.00. None of that information, taken together, is sufficient to tell a patient anything. The clinical work is: is the patient symptomatic, is the variant heterozygous or homozygous (CF is autosomal recessive, so a single copy is a carrier, not affected), what is the family history, are there compound heterozygous variants elsewhere in CFTR, what is the sweat chloride test result, is there nasal potential difference data. None of those are in your VCF. None of those are in your annotation report. The annotation report is a starting point, not a conclusion. We will repeat this point in Lecture 1, Lecture 3, and the mini-project read-me, because the most common mistake students make in Week 8 is treating "VEP said missense" as a clinical finding. It is not. It is a hypothesis to be tested by clinical workup.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Describe** the three orthogonal axes of variant interpretation — functional consequence (what does the variant do to the protein), population frequency (how often is it seen in healthy people), and clinical knowledge (what has been published about this variant) — and name the canonical free database for each: VEP/SnpEff for functional consequence, gnomAD for population frequency, ClinVar/dbSNP for clinical knowledge.
- **Run** the Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor (VEP) on a small VCF locally, against a downloaded VEP cache (no internet at run time), and read the resulting annotated VCF or JSON output. Identify the `CSQ` INFO field, the pipe-separated annotation columns, and the `Consequence`, `IMPACT`, `SIFT`, `PolyPhen`, and `Existing_variation` fields.
- **Run** SnpEff as a second annotator on the same VCF and confirm the two agree on consequence type for ~95% of variants. Identify the `ANN` INFO field and the four-letter impact code (`HIGH`, `MODERATE`, `LOW`, `MODIFIER`).
- **Parse** an annotated VCF in Python with `cyvcf2`. Extract the CSQ / ANN field, split on pipes, build a per-variant dict, and write a tabular per-variant summary to CSV.
- **Query** ClinVar, dbSNP, and gnomAD programmatically. Use the public NCBI E-utilities for ClinVar/dbSNP and the gnomAD GraphQL API for population frequency. Both are free, both are rate-limited, both have a Python client convention worth memorizing (`requests` + a small retry loop + a 200 ms sleep between calls; you wrote the pattern in Week 5).
- **Compute and interpret** SIFT and PolyPhen-2 scores. SIFT is a sequence-conservation-based score (0.0 = deleterious, 1.0 = tolerated; the cutoff is 0.05). PolyPhen-2 is a structure-and-conservation-based score (0.0 = benign, 1.0 = probably damaging; the cutoff is 0.5 for `possibly_damaging` and 0.85 for `probably_damaging`). VEP can report both via plugin output; SnpEff has its own `dbNSFP` integration for an even richer score set.
- **Apply** the ACMG/AMP 2015 criteria where they are mechanically applicable. PVS1 (null variant in a gene where LOF is a known disease mechanism — VEP `IMPACT=HIGH` plus a curated gene list). PM2 (absent or rare in population databases — gnomAD allele frequency < 0.0001). PP3 (multiple lines of computational evidence support a deleterious effect — SIFT < 0.05 AND PolyPhen > 0.85). BA1 (allele frequency > 5% in any general population — gnomAD AF > 0.05). BS1 (allele frequency greater than expected for the disorder). Acknowledge which criteria require *manual* curation (PS3 functional studies, PS4 case-control increased prevalence, PM3 in trans with pathogenic, PP1 cosegregation) and cannot be mechanized.
- **Identify** the limitations and standard error modes of automated variant annotation: transcript-of-reference choice changes the consequence (Ensembl canonical vs MANE Select vs RefSeq Select), splicing predictions are noisy outside the canonical splice sites, the "missense" category lumps together very different effects, gnomAD frequencies are sub-population-dependent and the global allele frequency obscures founder effects, ClinVar has conflicting interpretations for ~3% of variants.
- **Write** a defensible per-variant interpretation report with a one-line summary per variant, the source databases and versions, the date of the run, and a "this is not a clinical interpretation" disclaimer.
- **Query** PharmGKB for variants in the well-characterized pharmacogenes (`CYP2D6`, `CYP2C19`, `CYP2C9`, `TPMT`, `DPYD`, `SLCO1B1`, `VKORC1`, `CYP3A5`, `UGT1A1`, `HLA-B`) and translate a variant call into a star-allele haplotype and a CPIC-derived drug-response phenotype. Note where star-allele calling fails (the long-range haplotype phasing problem) and what the limits are.

## Topics covered

- **The three axes of variant interpretation**: functional consequence (the change to the protein), population frequency (how common in healthy people), and clinical knowledge (what is published). Each axis has a canonical free public database and a canonical free tool to query it. Mixing the axes (e.g. treating a high-impact functional prediction as a clinical finding without checking population frequency) is the most common interpretation error.
- **VEP (Variant Effect Predictor)**: the Ensembl-maintained variant annotator. McLaren et al. 2016, *Genome Biology* 17:122. Free, open source, published as a Perl CLI plus a JSON-emitting Python-friendly mode. Annotates variants against the Ensembl gene model (typically GRCh38, but any species Ensembl supports). Output: an extended VCF with a `CSQ` INFO field that is pipe-separated and one record per overlapping transcript. The default output covers consequence type (missense_variant, synonymous_variant, stop_gained, frameshift_variant, splice_donor_variant, etc., per the Sequence Ontology), the affected transcript, the codon and amino acid change, IMPACT (HIGH / MODERATE / LOW / MODIFIER), and the existing rsID if known. Plugins extend the output with SIFT, PolyPhen-2, dbNSFP, MaxEntScan, SpliceAI, etc.
- **SnpEff**: an independent variant annotator. Cingolani et al. 2012, *Fly* 6:80. Free, open source, published as a Java jar. Annotates against a SnpEff database (one per genome build, downloaded once). Output: a VCF with an `ANN` INFO field, pipe-separated, similar in spirit to VEP's CSQ but with a slightly different schema and impact-grading scheme. SnpEff is faster than VEP and has a wider species library but its plugin ecosystem is smaller.
- **ClinVar**: the NCBI clinical-variant database. Landrum et al. 2018, *Nucleic Acids Research* 46:D1062. Public, free, ~3 million records as of 2024. Each record has a `CLNSIG` (clinical significance: Benign / Likely Benign / VUS / Likely Pathogenic / Pathogenic), a `CLNREVSTAT` (review status, 0-4 stars), and a `CLNDISDB` (associated disease ontology IDs). Query via the NCBI E-utilities (`efetch` against `clinvar`) or by joining on rsID in the bundled VCF release.
- **dbSNP**: the NCBI single-nucleotide-polymorphism database. Sherry et al. 2001, *Nucleic Acids Research* 29:308. Provides the canonical rsID for each variant. ~700 million variants in build 156. Used as the "what is this variant called in the literature" lookup. Query via E-utilities or by joining on coordinate in the bundled VCF.
- **gnomAD**: the broad-institute-maintained population frequency database. Karczewski et al. 2020, *Nature* 581:434. ~125,000 exomes plus ~76,000 genomes from healthy or "no severe pediatric disease" cohorts, broken down by sub-population (African / Latino / East Asian / non-Finnish European / Finnish / Ashkenazi Jewish / South Asian / other). Provides per-variant allele frequency, allele count, sub-population-stratified frequency, and a flag for high-confidence loss-of-function. Query via the GraphQL API at <https://gnomad.broadinstitute.org/api> or by downloading the bundled VCF (~50 GB for the full genome dataset; ~10 GB for the exome subset).
- **SIFT and PolyPhen-2**: the two canonical computational missense predictors. SIFT (Ng and Henikoff 2003, *Nucleic Acids Research* 31:3812) uses sequence conservation across orthologs: if the residue is conserved, a non-conservative substitution is predicted deleterious. PolyPhen-2 (Adzhubei et al. 2010, *Nature Methods* 7:248) adds structural context: solvent accessibility, secondary structure, predicted impact on protein stability. Both report a score in [0, 1]; SIFT calls < 0.05 "deleterious," PolyPhen-2 calls > 0.85 "probably damaging." Modern best practice uses both and treats agreement as a stronger signal than either alone.
- **ACMG/AMP 2015 classification**: Richards et al. 2015, *Genetics in Medicine* 17:405. Free full text at PubMed Central: <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4544753/>. The five-tier classification (Benign / Likely Benign / Variant of Uncertain Significance / Likely Pathogenic / Pathogenic) is produced by combining 28 criteria across four evidence categories: population data (BA, BS, PM2), computational/predictive data (PVS1, PP3, BP4), functional data (PS3, BS3), and segregation/case data (PS4, PM3, PP1). The criteria combine by a tabulated decision tree. Some criteria are mechanically applicable from a VCF + databases (PVS1, PM2, PP3, BA1, BS1, BP4); most are not. Week 8 implements the mechanically applicable subset and labels the rest as "requires manual review."
- **PharmGKB**: the Stanford-maintained pharmacogenomics knowledge base. Whirl-Carrillo et al. 2021, *Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics* 110:563. Free tier covers the ~50 most clinically relevant pharmacogenes and the ~30 CPIC-graded gene-drug pairs (`CYP2D6` / codeine, `CYP2C19` / clopidogrel, `TPMT` / 6-mercaptopurine, `DPYD` / 5-fluorouracil, `SLCO1B1` / simvastatin, `HLA-B*5701` / abacavir, etc.). A pharmacogenomics report takes a variant call, maps the variant to a "star allele" haplotype, looks up the gene-drug interaction in PharmGKB, and reports the CPIC-graded recommendation. Limits: star-allele calling requires long-range phasing that is fragile from short-read data, and many gene-drug pairs are not in the CPIC tier-1 list.
- **Standard pitfalls and how to avoid them**: transcript-of-reference choice (MANE Select vs Ensembl canonical), the difference between "predicted deleterious" and "clinically actionable" (essentially everything in this week), conflicting ClinVar submissions (~3% of records), gnomAD sub-population effects (a variant common in Finnish but rare globally is still common in Finnish patients), and the date stamp on every database (run-time databases drift weekly; pin versions).
- **Reproducibility hygiene for annotation**: every annotation run must record the VEP version, the VEP cache version (release 110 vs 111), the SnpEff database version, the ClinVar VCF release date, the gnomAD version (v2.1 vs v3.1 vs v4.1, all in the wild), and the date of the run. Without these, the same VCF can produce different annotations months apart and the difference is impossible to debug.

## A note on tone

C10 is written in **lab-notebook voice**. We pin versions ("VEP 110.1," "SnpEff 5.2," "ClinVar VCF release 2024-09-01," "gnomAD v4.1.0," "cyvcf2 0.30.28"). We cite tools by their paper ("VEP, McLaren et al. 2016 *Genome Biol* 17:122; SnpEff, Cingolani et al. 2012 *Fly* 6:80; ACMG 2015, Richards et al. *Genet Med* 17:405"). We say "VEP annotated 47 of 50 variants as `missense_variant` or `synonymous_variant`; 2 of 50 as `stop_gained` (one in `BRCA1`, one in `TP53`); 1 of 50 as `splice_donor_variant` in `CFTR` intron 19" not "VEP annotated the variants." A clinical-grade pipeline reports the version of every database it queried, the date of the run, and the consequence and frequency of every variant in a structured table. The numbers are the work.

## A note on the data size

A VCF with 50 hand-picked variants is a few KB. The VEP cache for human GRCh38 release 110 is ~25 GB on disk (it contains every transcript and protein sequence for the genome). The SnpEff GRCh38 database is ~2.5 GB. The full ClinVar VCF release is ~200 MB. The gnomAD v4.1 exome subset is ~10 GB; the full genome subset is ~50 GB. For the Week 8 exercises and mini-project we use the **small versions where they exist** and **query the GraphQL API where they do not**, so the on-disk footprint stays under 30 GB. If you do not have 30 GB free, swap in the GitHub Codespaces or Google Colab path documented in `resources.md`.

Commit the input VCF, the annotated VCF, the per-variant report CSV/HTML, and the run-info JSON. Gitignore the raw VEP cache (too large) and any intermediate files. For a clinical-grade pipeline, the run-info JSON is the *most important* artifact in the directory — without it, the annotation cannot be reproduced.

## Stretch goals

If you finish early and want to push further, try any of the following:

- Read the ACMG/AMP 2015 paper end to end (Richards et al. 2015 — free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4544753/>) and the 2023 SVI updates (Walsh et al. 2023 for PVS1, Biesecker et al. 2024 for PP3/BP4). The 2015 paper is the foundation; the updates have refined ~6 of the 28 criteria over the last decade.
- Add **SpliceAI** (Jaganathan et al. 2019, *Cell* 176:535) as a VEP plugin. SpliceAI is a free, pretrained convolutional neural network that scores any variant for its likelihood of disrupting a splice site, including non-canonical ones far from the GT-AG dinucleotides. It is the modern complement to MaxEntScan for splice prediction.
- Add **CADD** (Rentzsch et al. 2019, *Nucleic Acids Research* 47:D886) as a third in-silico predictor. CADD combines ~60 features into a single deleteriousness score, scaled so that score >= 20 means "in the top 1% of likely-deleterious variants." Adds to the PP3/BP4 evidence.
- Annotate a multi-sample VCF (e.g. the GIAB NA12878 + NA24385 + NA24149 trio) and produce a per-trio inheritance pattern column (de novo, autosomal recessive, autosomal dominant, X-linked). The inheritance pattern is what most clinical pipelines pivot on.
- Reproduce a published variant interpretation from a clinical case report end to end. The OMIM database has thousands of well-described variants. Pick one, run your pipeline, and see how close you get to the published interpretation. The gap is where the clinical judgment lives.
