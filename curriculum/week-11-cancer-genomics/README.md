# Week 11 — Cancer Genomics: Somatic vs Germline Analysis

> **Educational and research use only.** This week builds and reasons about somatic variant-calling pipelines on simulated or public anonymized data. **Nothing in this week is clinical software, none of it is validated for diagnostic use, and no output of any pipeline here may be used to guide patient care.** Clinical somatic variant calling is the work of accredited laboratories operating under CAP / CLIA (United States), ISO 15189 (international), or equivalent oversight; their pipelines are locked, version-pinned, periodically re-validated, and audited. The Week 11 deliverables exist to teach the *mechanics* of somatic variant calling — Mutect2, Strelka2, FilterMutectCalls, contamination estimation, mutational signatures, the COSMIC / OncoKB / CIViC interpretation layer — not to substitute for a clinical assay. Treat every result in this week as a learning exercise. Do not share patient-identifiable data with this code. If a real sample's analysis is at stake, route it to a clinical lab.

> **Reproducibility note.** Somatic variant calling is randomized in fewer places than long-read assembly but the inputs are far larger and the analytic chain is longer. Mutect2 itself is deterministic on a fixed BAM pair (given the same reference, the same intervals, and the same PON), but the inputs upstream are not: the basecaller, the aligner, the duplicate marker, and the base-quality recalibration step all introduce variation that propagates downstream. The Week 11 deliverables pin every tool version, every reference genome build (GRCh38 / hg38, not GRCh37), the panel-of-normals (PON) source, the germline-resource VCF (gnomAD), the FilterMutectCalls thresholds, the COSMIC release, and the SigProfiler reference signatures version. Without that pinned chain, two pipelines run on the same FASTQ pair can produce VCFs that disagree on 5-15% of low-allele-frequency calls and the disagreement is impossible to debug.

In Week 10 you took raw long reads and produced a polished assembly: `badread` for the simulated reads, Flye for the OLC assembly, Medaka for the polish, BUSCO and N50 for the QC, and a `run-info.json` that travels with the FASTA. The unit of analysis was the long read; the output was a set of contigs. Week 11 leaves *de novo* assembly behind and asks a different question that uses many of the same primitives (BAM, subprocess, version pinning, run-info JSON): **given a tumor sample sequenced alongside a matched normal sample from the same patient, which variants are somatic (acquired by the tumor) and which are germline (inherited by the patient)?** The unit of analysis is the tumor-normal matched pair; the output is a filtered, annotated VCF and a mutational signature decomposition.

The Week 1-10 single-sample assumption breaks here. Cancer is a clonal disease: a tumor begins as one cell that has acquired one or more driver mutations (TP53, KRAS, BRAF, the cancer-gene panel of the field), and as the tumor grows the clone accumulates passenger mutations on top of the drivers. Every cancer cell in a tumor sample shares its germline variants with every normal cell in the same patient — those came from the patient's parents at conception, are present in every cell of the body, and are not the cause of the cancer (with rare exceptions: BRCA1/2 carriers, Lynch syndrome). The variants that are present in the tumor but **not** in the matched normal sample are the somatic mutations. The whole framing of Week 11 is the comparison: tumor minus normal equals tumor-specific.

The matched-pair model has been the standard for somatic variant calling since the 2010s and is implemented in every modern caller — **Mutect2** (Cibulskis et al. 2013, *Nature Biotechnology* 31:213; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3833702/>; the original Mutect was the 2013 publication; Mutect2 is the rewritten version inside the Broad Institute's Genome Analysis Toolkit, GATK), **Strelka2** (Kim et al. 2018, *Nature Methods* 15:591; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6314977/>), **VarScan2**, **Lancet**, **DeepVariant + somatic-mode**, and a long tail of academic tools. We pick two — Mutect2 and Strelka2 — and use Mutect2 as the default. Both are free and open source. Both are deterministic given the same inputs. Both produce VCF output that downstream tools (Funcotator, VEP, SnpEff, OncoKB, CIViC) can annotate.

By Friday of Week 11 you will be able to take a tumor-normal BAM pair (we ship a small simulated pair as the didactic dataset), run Mutect2 in tumor-normal mode via subprocess, run FilterMutectCalls with a pinned set of thresholds, optionally cross-check with Strelka2, estimate the tumor contamination with `CalculateContamination` (which uses gnomAD allele frequencies; free public resource), parse the filtered VCF, count single-nucleotide variants (SNVs) by their trinucleotide context, decompose the SNV spectrum into the COSMIC v3.3 mutational signatures with deconstructSigs (free, open source) or SigProfilerAssignment (free, open source, the canonical reference), and emit a Markdown report that names the top three signatures and their cosine similarity to the catalog. The mini-project wraps the whole pipeline as a CLI script with a `run-info.json` that pins every parameter.

The other half of the week is **how the call can be wrong**. Somatic variant calling is a noisier inference than germline calling for several reasons. (a) Tumors are heterogeneous: a 10% allele-frequency variant may be present in only a sub-clone of the tumor, and at low coverage it is indistinguishable from a sequencing error. (b) Tumors and normals are not always equally clean: a "normal" blood sample from a leukemia patient is, in fact, leukemic; a "normal" tissue near a tumor may have field-effect mutations. (c) Tumor purity is rarely 100%: a sample sent to the lab is a mixture of tumor cells, infiltrating immune cells, and stroma; if the tumor is 30% of the sample, the apparent allele frequencies are scaled down by 0.3. (d) Tumor-normal contamination is real: lab cross-contamination of ~1% from a high-allele-frequency germline variant produces a "somatic" signal at ~1% allele frequency that is, in fact, a germline variant from another patient. The Mutect2 `CalculateContamination` step is the standard way to estimate (d) and the `--contamination-table` flag passes the estimate to FilterMutectCalls. Lecture 1 and Lecture 3 spell these out; the mini-project write-up has to name them.

The interpretation layer is the third half (a week has three halves now). A filtered VCF of somatic SNVs is a starting point, not an answer. **COSMIC** (Catalogue Of Somatic Mutations In Cancer; Sondka et al. 2024, *Nucleic Acids Research* 52:D1210; free for academic use at <https://cancer.sanger.ac.uk/cosmic>) is the canonical catalog of recurrent somatic mutations and the source of the v3.x mutational-signature reference set. **OncoKB** (Chakravarty et al. 2017, *JCO Precision Oncology* 1:1; free at <https://www.oncokb.org/>) maps somatic variants to FDA evidence levels for actionable cancer therapies; the public tier covers the standard panel of clinically actionable alterations. **CIViC** (Griffith et al. 2017, *Nature Genetics* 49:170; free at <https://civicdb.org/>) is the community-curated knowledge base of variant-level clinical evidence. None of these turns a research VCF into clinical advice, but they turn a list of variants into a *prioritized* list of variants worth a human's attention, and they make plain which evidence levels each variant has reached.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Describe** the matched tumor-normal model of somatic variant calling: tumor sample plus matched normal sample from the same patient; the goal is the set difference (variants in tumor but not in normal); the model assumes the normal sample is genuinely a non-tumor tissue from the same individual and that the tumor sample is enriched for tumor cells. Name two ways the assumption can fail: contamination across samples and tumor-in-normal contamination (a leukemia patient's blood normal contains leukemia).
- **Run** Mutect2 from the command line and from Python via `subprocess.run(..., check=True, capture_output=True)`. Pin `-R` (reference), `-I` (tumor and normal BAM, with `-tumor` and `-normal` sample-name flags), `--panel-of-normals` (a PON VCF distilled from technically-similar normal samples; free PONs are published by the Broad Institute), `--germline-resource` (gnomAD allele frequencies, free at <https://gnomad.broadinstitute.org/>), and an interval file for restricted runs. Verify that two runs on the same BAM pair with the same PON produce a byte-identical raw VCF.
- **Run** FilterMutectCalls to apply the standard filter set (germline likelihood, strand bias, base-quality bias, weak evidence, position-in-read bias, contamination, normal-artifact). Read the resulting VCF and count PASS variants; count each filter reason; name which filter caught which variants.
- **Run** Strelka2 (Kim et al. 2018) as an independent cross-check on the same BAM pair. Note that Strelka2 has its own filter set and emits SNVs and indels in separate VCFs; compare the PASS-PASS overlap with Mutect2 (the agreement rate is typically 80-95% at AF >= 10%, lower at AF < 5%).
- **Estimate** tumor purity and contamination with GATK's `GetPileupSummaries` + `CalculateContamination`. State the typical contamination range (well-handled samples: 0-2%; flagged samples: 5%+) and the typical purity range (clean tumors: 60-90%; low-purity samples: 20-40%, requiring careful interpretation of low-AF variants).
- **Reason about** copy-number variation (CNV) at the conceptual level: tumors lose, gain, and amplify chromosomal regions; copy-number callers (GATK CNV, FACETS, Sequenza, CNVkit) estimate the copy-number state per genomic bin from the read-depth ratio of tumor to normal, often co-modeled with the B-allele frequency of heterozygous SNPs. We do not run a CNV caller this week; we explain why a CNV call changes the *interpretation* of a SNV (e.g. a TP53 mutation in a region with loss-of-heterozygosity is a different disease than a TP53 mutation in a diploid region).
- **Extract** the trinucleotide context of each somatic SNV (the base, the base before it, the base after it; the strand convention is C>T / A>G — every mutation is normalized to the pyrimidine reference) and count the 96 distinct mutation classes. The 96-class spectrum is the input to mutational-signature decomposition.
- **Decompose** the 96-class spectrum into the COSMIC v3.3 reference signatures using deconstructSigs (Rosenthal et al. 2016, *Genome Biology* 17:31; <https://github.com/raerose01/deconstructSigs>) or SigProfilerAssignment (Alexandrov et al. 2020, *Nature* 578:94; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7054213/>; the canonical reference for COSMIC v3). Read the output: each signature gets a fractional contribution (sums to 1.0); the residual is the unmodelled fraction; the cosine similarity between the reconstructed and observed spectra is the goodness-of-fit. Cite the active-signature interpretation: SBS1 (age-related), SBS5 (clock-like), SBS4 (tobacco smoking), SBS7a/b/c/d (UV), SBS6 / SBS15 / SBS20 / SBS26 (mismatch repair deficiency), SBS3 (homologous-recombination deficiency, BRCA-pathway loss).
- **Query** the COSMIC, OncoKB, and CIViC databases for clinical-interpretation context. We do not auto-call clinical evidence — we look up a small number of variants by hand or via the public APIs and report what each database says about each variant's evidence level (FDA-approved, off-label, preclinical, mechanistic).
- **Build** the full pipeline as a CLI script: tumor BAM and normal BAM in (we ship a small didactic pair), Mutect2 + FilterMutectCalls, optional Strelka2 cross-check, optional `CalculateContamination` step, parse the PASS VCF, render the 96-class spectrum, decompose into COSMIC v3.3 signatures, emit a Markdown report and a `run-info.json`.
- **Identify and reason about** the standard failure modes: low tumor purity (apparent allele frequencies scaled; low-AF true positives lost in the noise floor), tumor-in-normal contamination (true positives filtered out by the normal-artifact filter), cross-sample contamination (false positives appear at the contaminant's germline allele frequency), reference-build drift (a pipeline pinned to GRCh38 will silently produce coordinate-shifted calls on GRCh37 input), and the signature decomposition's degeneracy (some signatures are co-linear — SBS3 and SBS39 are notoriously hard to separate without sufficient mutation counts).

## Topics covered

- **The tumor-normal matched pair.** The fundamental data type of this week. Tumor BAM plus normal BAM, both aligned to the same reference, sorted, duplicate-marked, base-quality-recalibrated. The sample names in the BAM `@RG` headers must be set correctly so that the caller can distinguish them; pipelines that mis-tag tumor and normal produce inverted calls.
- **Mutect2** (Cibulskis et al. 2013, *Nat Biotechnol* 31:213; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3833702/>). The 2013 paper describes the original Mutect; GATK's Mutect2 is the rewrite that adds joint indel calling, the panel-of-normals (PON) idea, and the matched-normal model. Mutect2 is the de facto default for matched tumor-normal somatic SNV + indel calling. It outputs an unfiltered VCF that downstream `FilterMutectCalls` annotates with filter reasons.
- **Strelka2** (Kim et al. 2018, *Nat Methods* 15:591; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6314977/>). An independent matched tumor-normal caller from Illumina. Different statistical model, different filter heuristics, similar overall sensitivity and specificity. The standard practice is to call with both and report variants that PASS in either or in both, depending on the project's tolerance for false positives vs false negatives.
- **The Panel of Normals (PON).** A multi-sample VCF distilled from many technically-similar normal samples; variants present in the PON at non-trivial frequency are flagged as recurrent technical artefacts (alignment errors near hard-to-map regions, systematic base-calling errors near homopolymers) and Mutect2 down-weights or filters them. The Broad publishes free PONs derived from gnomAD-WES and gnomAD-WGS normals; for a project sequenced on a non-standard platform you should ideally build your own PON from internal controls.
- **The germline-resource VCF.** A precomputed allele-frequency annotation for known germline variants; gnomAD is the canonical source (Karczewski et al. 2020, *Nature* 581:434; free at <https://gnomad.broadinstitute.org/>). Mutect2 uses it to compute the probability that a "candidate somatic" variant is in fact a known germline variant at non-trivial population frequency; FilterMutectCalls uses the same annotation to filter likely germline contamination.
- **FilterMutectCalls and the filter set.** The standard filter set: `germline`, `panel_of_normals`, `clustered_events`, `multiallelic`, `weak_evidence`, `strand_bias`, `base_qual`, `map_qual`, `position`, `contamination`, `n_ratio`, `read_position`, `fragment_length`, `slippage`. Each variant in the VCF emerges with one of `PASS` or a comma-separated list of filter reasons in the FILTER column. The Week 11 pipeline reports the count of each.
- **Tumor purity and contamination.** Two distinct concepts. Purity is the fraction of the sample that is tumor cells (the rest is stroma, infiltrating immune cells, necrotic tissue, blood); contamination is the fraction of the sample that is genetic material from a *different individual* (lab cross-contamination, sample-mixup). GATK `GetPileupSummaries` + `CalculateContamination` estimates contamination via the allele-frequency distribution of common variants at known-heterozygous sites in the normal; the output is a `contamination.table` that FilterMutectCalls consumes via `--contamination-table`.
- **Copy-number variation (CNV) at the conceptual level.** CNV callers estimate copy-number state per genomic bin from the log2 read-depth ratio of tumor to normal, often co-modeled with the B-allele frequency of heterozygous SNPs. CNVs change the *interpretation* of a SNV: a TP53 mutation with loss-of-heterozygosity (one mutant allele + deletion of the wild-type allele) is biallelic loss of TP53 function, which is the disease-causing event; a TP53 mutation in a diploid region with both alleles present is monoallelic and may be a passenger. The Week 11 mini-project does not call CNVs (each tool has its own substantial setup cost) but the discussion section names the consequences for SNV interpretation.
- **The trinucleotide context and the 96-class spectrum.** Every SNV has a reference base and an alternate base. With six possible substitution types (C>A, C>G, C>T, T>A, T>C, T>G after normalizing every mutation to the pyrimidine reference) and four possible bases on each side of the substitution (A, C, G, T), the total is 6 * 4 * 4 = 96 trinucleotide-context-specific mutation classes. Plotting the count of each class in a sample produces the **96-class mutational spectrum**, the input to signature decomposition.
- **COSMIC v3 mutational signatures** (Alexandrov et al. 2020, *Nature* 578:94; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7054213/>). The Alexandrov-lab catalog of recurrent mutational signatures derived from thousands of pan-cancer whole-genome sequences. v3.3 (the current at time of writing) contains 78 single-base substitution (SBS) signatures, plus indel (ID) and double-base (DBS) signatures. Each signature is a probability distribution over the 96 classes; each tumor's observed spectrum is decomposed as a linear combination of signatures plus residual. The canonical signatures: SBS1 (5-methylcytosine deamination, clock-like with age), SBS5 (clock-like, unknown source), SBS4 (tobacco), SBS7a-d (UV), SBS6 / SBS15 / SBS20 / SBS26 (mismatch repair), SBS3 (homologous recombination deficiency), SBS10a/b (polymerase epsilon hypermutation), SBS9 (POLH activity / lymphoid hypermutation).
- **deconstructSigs and SigProfilerAssignment.** Two methods for assigning a tumor's spectrum to the COSMIC catalog. deconstructSigs (Rosenthal et al. 2016, *Genome Biology* 17:31) is older, written in R, uses iterative least-squares; SigProfilerAssignment (Alexandrov-lab, 2020+) is the canonical reference, written in Python, supports the full COSMIC v3 catalog and additional signatures, and uses non-negative least squares with a complementary cross-validation. Both are free and open source. We use SigProfilerAssignment in the pipeline because it is Python-native and ships the COSMIC v3 catalog directly.
- **COSMIC, OncoKB, CIViC.** Three clinical-interpretation knowledge bases. COSMIC (Sondka et al. 2024, *Nucleic Acids Research* 52:D1210; free for academic use at <https://cancer.sanger.ac.uk/cosmic>) is the catalog of recurrent somatic variants. OncoKB (Chakravarty et al. 2017, *JCO Precision Oncology* 1:1; <https://www.oncokb.org/>) maps variants to FDA evidence levels for targeted therapies (Level 1: FDA-approved in this tumor type; Level 2: standard care; Level 3A/B: clinical evidence; Level 4: biological evidence). CIViC (Griffith et al. 2017, *Nature Genetics* 49:170; <https://civicdb.org/>) is the community-curated variant-evidence database. We use these as look-up references, not as auto-callers.
- **Reproducibility hygiene for cancer pipelines.** Pin every tool version (GATK 4.5.0.0, Strelka 2.9.10, samtools 1.20, bcftools 1.20, SigProfilerAssignment 0.1.4). Pin the reference build (GRCh38 specifically; never silently mix GRCh37 and GRCh38). Pin the PON source and date. Pin the gnomAD release used as `--germline-resource`. Pin the FilterMutectCalls thresholds (the `--min-allele-fraction`, the contamination threshold). Pin the COSMIC signature catalog release (v3.3 specifically). Without these, two runs of the same pipeline on the same BAM pair can produce different VCFs and you will not be able to reproduce a published call.
- **Standard failure modes.** Low tumor purity (apparent AFs scaled down; low-AF true positives lost). Tumor-in-normal contamination (the normal contains tumor signal; true positives filtered as germline). Cross-sample contamination (false positives at a contaminant's germline AF). Reference-build drift (GRCh37 vs GRCh38 produces silently wrong coordinates). Panel-of-normals mismatch (a PON from a different platform leaves platform-specific artefacts unfiltered). Signature degeneracy (low mutation counts cannot distinguish SBS3 from SBS39; report the cosine similarity and confidence interval). The mini-project write-up names which of these its pipeline could plausibly suffer from.

## A note on the data size

A real whole-genome tumor-normal pair is 60-100 GB of BAMs (one per sample at 30-60x coverage). A whole-exome pair is 5-15 GB. The Week 11 didactic dataset is much smaller: a 1.4 Mb chr22 subset of a public simulated tumor-normal pair, with the matched reference (chr22 only of GRCh38) and a chr22-restricted PON and gnomAD VCF. The end-to-end pipeline runs in 5-15 minutes on a 16 GB laptop. The mini-project ramps the input up to a 10 Mb subset (chr17 + chr22), which runs in 30-60 minutes.

Commit the input BAMs (small), the reference subset, the PON subset, the gnomAD subset, the raw Mutect2 VCF, the filtered VCF, the contamination table, the 96-class spectrum TSV, the signature-assignment TSV, the Markdown report, and the `run-info.json`. Gitignore the GATK intermediate logs, the Strelka working directory (large; reproducible), and any large reference files that exceed 100 MB (download via a `setup.sh` script that pulls from the Broad Institute resource bundle).

- Read the Mutect2 paper (Cibulskis et al. 2013) end to end at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3833702/>. The Bayesian model in §2 is the technical core and explains why the matched-normal model is more sensitive than a no-normal somatic caller for low-AF variants.
- Read the COSMIC signatures paper (Alexandrov et al. 2020) at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7054213/>. The non-negative matrix factorization formulation in §2-§3 is the math behind every signature method; the v3 catalog is the practical reference set.
- Run a CNV caller (CNVkit on the same BAM pair: `cnvkit.py batch tumor.bam --normal normal.bam --output-dir cnv_out --processes 4`). Inspect the resulting `.cns` segments; identify any region of loss-of-heterozygosity overlapping a Mutect2 PASS variant.
- Build a small in-house PON from three of the didactic-pair normals (combine three normal BAMs with `Mutect2 --tumor` and then `CreateSomaticPanelOfNormals`). Compare PASS-variant counts against the same pipeline run with the public Broad PON.
- Compute the trinucleotide spectrum for a known smoking-associated lung cancer (use one of the PCAWG sample profiles publicly distributed by the COSMIC project; e.g. `SP116816`) and verify that SBS4 (the tobacco signature) is recovered with cosine similarity > 0.95.
- Annotate the PASS VCF with Funcotator (`gatk Funcotator ... --data-sources-path /path/to/funcotator/dataSourcesFolder --output-file annotated.vcf.gz`) and look up the top three most-frequently-cited cancer genes (TP53, KRAS, BRAF) in the annotated VCF.
