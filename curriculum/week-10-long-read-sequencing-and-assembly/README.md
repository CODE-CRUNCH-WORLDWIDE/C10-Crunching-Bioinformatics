# Week 10 — Long-Read Sequencing and De Novo Assembly

> **Reproducibility note.** Long-read assembly is randomized at several layers: the basecaller emits slightly different reads from the same raw signal depending on the model version; the assembler's internal graph-cleaning passes use a random seed for tie-breaking on equivalent overlaps; the polisher's neural network has a fixed weight set but a stochastic batching layer. Two runs of the same pipeline on the same FAST5 / POD5 archive can produce assemblies that differ at a handful of bases — and, on highly repetitive regions, can produce contigs of different lengths. The Week 10 deliverables pin every parameter (Dorado model name and version, Flye version, polish tool and version, the assembly random seed, and the read-quality filter threshold) and emit them in a `run-info.json` that travels with every FASTA contig set. Without that JSON, two assemblies that look identical may have been built from subtly different intermediates and you will not be able to reconstruct which was which.

In Week 9 you took a FASTA of homologous sequences and produced a tree: MAFFT for the alignment, neighbor-joining or IQ-TREE 2 for the topology, bootstrap support for the branches, and a `run-info.json` that travels with the Newick. The unit of analysis was the alignment column; the output was a tree. Week 10 leaves comparative analysis behind and asks the upstream question: **given the raw output of a sequencing instrument, how do you reconstruct the genome the reads came from when there is no reference?** The unit of analysis is the long read; the output is a set of contigs in FASTA format. This is **de novo assembly**, and the tool surface is different from everything in Weeks 1-9.

The Week 1-8 read assumption was Illumina short reads: 150-300 bp, very low per-base error rate (~0.1%), and the assumption that a reference genome already exists. Week 10 changes both ends of that assumption. The reads are long (5,000 to over 100,000 bp on a single read; ~30,000 bp on average for Oxford Nanopore R10.4 chemistry; ~15,000-20,000 bp for PacBio HiFi). The per-base error rate is higher on raw nanopore reads (~5-10% depending on chemistry; ~1-2% after Dorado basecalling under the latest models) and lower on PacBio HiFi (~0.1-0.2%; the consensus is built from circular-consensus passes over the same molecule). And there is no reference: the input is just FASTQ, and the goal is to build the reference from scratch.

The Week 10 framing is deliberately narrow. We pick one assembler family (the overlap-layout-consensus assemblers — **Flye**, Kolmogorov et al. 2019, *Nature Biotechnology* 37:540; and **Canu**, Koren et al. 2017, *Genome Research* 27:722), one HiFi-specialized assembler (**Hifiasm**, Cheng et al. 2021, *Nature Methods* 18:170), one polisher per platform (**Medaka** for nanopore; **DeepConsensus** for HiFi), one QC suite (**BUSCO**, Manni et al. 2021, *Molecular Biology and Evolution* 38:4647; **Bandage**, Wick et al. 2015, *Bioinformatics* 31:3350; and the `asmstats` / `N50` calculation by hand), and one read simulator (**badread**, Wick 2019, *Journal of Open Source Software* 4:1316). All of those are free, open source, and pip- or conda-installable. The sequencing chemistries we name (Oxford Nanopore R10.4.1, PacBio HiFi from Sequel II / Revio) are public; the basecaller (**Dorado**, Oxford Nanopore Technologies) is open source under a custom license.

By Friday of Week 10 you will be able to take a simulated FASTQ of long reads from a 1 Mb genome, filter the reads by length and quality, run **Flye** to assemble them into contigs, polish the contigs with **Medaka** (for nanopore reads) or skip polishing (for HiFi reads), compute the assembly statistics (number of contigs, longest contig length, N50, L50, total bases assembled), run **BUSCO** to estimate the fraction of single-copy orthologs that are present and complete, and report the whole pipeline in a `run-info.json`. The mini-project is the whole pipeline wrapped in a CLI script, with the read simulation parameters and the assembly parameters pinned, and the assembly QC report rendered as a Markdown summary.

The other half of the week is **why the assembly might be wrong**, and the answer has many layers. The reads can be wrong (basecalling errors, especially in homopolymer runs; chimeric reads where two distant fragments are read as one molecule; adapter contamination at the read ends). The assembly graph can be wrong (repetitive regions collapse into a single contig when they are really N copies; long repeats wider than the read length cannot be resolved without ultra-long reads or Hi-C scaffolding). The polishing can be wrong (Medaka's neural network is trained on specific chemistries and basecaller models; a mismatch between the training data and the input data produces systematic errors). And the QC can mislead (BUSCO scores are gene-content-only and miss large-scale structural problems; N50 alone is a poor summary statistic because it can be inflated by collapsed repeats). We name these limits in Lecture 2 and in the mini-project write-up; you will repeat them whenever you publish an assembly.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Describe** the difference between Oxford Nanopore and PacBio HiFi sequencing in terms of read length distribution, per-base error rate, error mode (random vs systematic), and the trade-off each platform makes between cost per base, run time, and downstream consensus accuracy. Name the R10.4.1 chemistry (the current ONT default) and the CCS / HiFi pipeline (PacBio's circular consensus sequencing).
- **Run** Flye from the command line and from Python via `subprocess.run(..., check=True, capture_output=True)`. Pin the `--nano-hq` or `--pacbio-hifi` input mode, pass an estimated genome size with `--genome-size`, set the thread count, and verify that a second run with the same input and the same seed produces a byte-equal assembly FASTA.
- **Run** Canu as an alternative OLC assembler and compare its output to Flye on the same input. State the trade-off: Canu is older, slower, and more memory-hungry, but its best-overlap-graph stage emits a more interpretable assembly graph for highly repetitive genomes.
- **Run** Hifiasm on PacBio HiFi reads and describe why a HiFi-specialized assembler beats a general-purpose OLC assembler on HiFi input: the low error rate lets Hifiasm skip the error-correction stage and emit haplotype-resolved contigs directly.
- **Parse** a FASTA contig set with `Bio.SeqIO`, compute the number of contigs, the longest contig length, the total bases, the **N50**, the L50, and the GC fraction. Implement the N50 / L50 calculation by hand (sort contigs descending by length, walk the cumulative sum until 50% of the total bases is reached; N50 is the length of the contig at that boundary; L50 is its rank).
- **Polish** an ONT assembly with Medaka (Oxford Nanopore Technologies; <https://github.com/nanoporetech/medaka>). Pin the Medaka model to the basecaller model that produced the reads (mismatched models produce worse polishes than no polish at all). State the typical polish improvement: a Flye assembly polished with one round of Medaka gains ~5-10x lower per-base error on a well-covered region.
- **Polish** a PacBio HiFi assembly with DeepConsensus (Baid et al. 2023, *Nature Biotechnology* 41:232; <https://github.com/google/deepconsensus>) at the read level, then re-assemble with Hifiasm. Note that HiFi assemblies often need no post-assembly polish; the polish is upstream at the read consensus step.
- **Run** BUSCO (Manni et al. 2021, *Mol Biol Evol* 38:4647) on the polished assembly. Pick a lineage dataset that matches the organism (e.g. `bacteria_odb10` for *E. coli*; `vertebrata_odb10` for a fish). Read the BUSCO summary: percentage of complete single-copy, complete duplicated, fragmented, and missing orthologs. Report all four.
- **Visualize** the assembly graph in **Bandage** (Wick et al. 2015, *Bioinformatics* 31:3350; <https://rrwick.github.io/Bandage/>). Identify the topology of the graph: a single linear path (cleanly assembled), a circular path (cleanly assembled bacterial chromosome), a bubble (heterozygous diploid region or sequencing-error artefact), a tangle (a repeat region the assembler could not resolve).
- **Build** the full pipeline as a CLI script: FASTQ in (simulated by `badread` from a known reference); read filter; Flye; Medaka polish (ONT) or no polish (HiFi); asmstats; BUSCO; render a Markdown QC summary; emit `run-info.json`. Run end-to-end in under 10 minutes on a 1 Mb genome on a laptop.
- **Identify and reason about** the standard failure modes: undercollapsed repeats (the assembler emits N tandem copies as N contigs when they are really N copies of one repeat in a row), overcollapsed repeats (the assembler emits one contig where the true sequence has K copies; this inflates N50 and deflates the assembly length), chimeric contigs (two distant regions joined by a low-confidence overlap), basecaller-version drift, and the model-mismatch problem in polishing.

## Topics covered

- **The two long-read platforms.** Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) drives a single strand of DNA through a protein pore embedded in a membrane and measures the ionic current as each base passes; the current trace is called the **squiggle** and a neural network (the basecaller) translates the squiggle into a base sequence. PacBio Single Molecule Real Time (SMRT) sequencing attaches a polymerase to the bottom of a zeptolitre well and watches the polymerase incorporate fluorescently-labelled nucleotides; the resulting raw reads are CLR (continuous long reads); the **HiFi** mode passes the same molecule through the polymerase multiple times (the **circular consensus sequencing** or CCS protocol) and emits a consensus read with ~0.1% error rate. The two platforms occupy different points on the error / length / cost trade-off and the tooling differs accordingly.
- **Oxford Nanopore R10.4 / R10.4.1 chemistry** (current as of 2024-2025). The R10.4 nanopore is a double-pore design that improves the signal-to-noise ratio over R9.4, particularly in homopolymer regions. The current Dorado basecaller's `dna_r10.4.1_e8.2_400bps_sup` model produces ~99% accurate basecalls on most regions. The squiggle is recorded as POD5 (the modern raw signal format; FAST5 is the older HDF5-based format).
- **Dorado** (Oxford Nanopore Technologies; <https://github.com/nanoporetech/dorado>) is the modern basecaller. It is GPU-accelerated; on a consumer GPU it basecalls ~5-10x faster than the older Guppy basecaller. The model is selected via `dorado basecaller dna_r10.4.1_e8.2_400bps_sup pod5/ > reads.fastq`. The `_sup` model is the "super-accuracy" model; the `_hac` model is "high-accuracy"; the `_fast` model is fastest. Always record which model produced the reads in any downstream `run-info.json`.
- **PacBio HiFi** (Wenger et al. 2019, *Nature Biotechnology* 37:1155; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6776680/>). The HiFi reads are the consensus of multiple passes over the same circular template; the consensus accuracy is ~99.8-99.9% on a typical run. The reads are ~15,000-20,000 bp; the per-base quality scores are calibrated and reliable (unlike raw ONT reads, where the early-version quality scores were known to be miscalibrated).
- **Overlap-Layout-Consensus (OLC) assembly.** The classical long-read assembly paradigm: (a) find all read-pair overlaps above a length / identity threshold; (b) layout the reads into a graph where nodes are reads and edges are overlaps; (c) walk the graph to produce contigs and call a consensus per column. **Flye** (Kolmogorov et al. 2019, *Nature Biotechnology* 37:540; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6699608/>) is the modern default for ONT and PacBio CLR reads; it uses a **repeat graph** internally to handle repetitive regions explicitly. **Canu** (Koren et al. 2017, *Genome Research* 27:722; free at <https://genome.cshlp.org/content/27/5/722>) is the older OLC assembler and uses a **best-overlap-graph** representation; it is slower and more memory-hungry but emits a more interpretable graph for highly repetitive genomes.
- **Hifiasm** (Cheng et al. 2021, *Nature Methods* 18:170; free at <https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-020-01056-5>) is specialized for PacBio HiFi reads. Because HiFi reads are already ~99.8% accurate, Hifiasm skips the error-correction stage that Flye and Canu spend most of their compute on; instead it builds the assembly graph directly from raw HiFi reads and emits haplotype-resolved primary and alternate contig sets in a single pass. On a yeast or *E. coli* genome from HiFi input, Hifiasm runs in ~5 minutes on a 16-core laptop.
- **Polishing with Medaka** (Oxford Nanopore Technologies; <https://github.com/nanoporetech/medaka>). After Flye emits a contig set from ONT reads, the per-base error rate is typically 0.5-2.0% (better than the raw reads but worse than HiFi). Medaka realigns the original reads to the assembly and uses a neural network trained on the same basecaller model to call a per-base consensus, typically improving the per-base error rate by ~5-10x. The Medaka model must match the basecaller model: pinning matters.
- **Polishing with DeepConsensus** (Baid et al. 2023, *Nature Biotechnology* 41:232; <https://github.com/google/deepconsensus>). DeepConsensus operates upstream of HiFi assembly: it takes the raw subreads (multiple passes over the same circular template) and produces a higher-quality consensus read than the standard CCS algorithm. It is a transformer-based model trained on a large HiFi training set; running it adds ~1-2 hours on a large GPU but typically lifts the HiFi accuracy from ~99.8% to ~99.95%. The reassembled contigs are correspondingly cleaner.
- **Assembly QC.** The conventional summary statistic is **N50**: sort contigs descending by length; the N50 is the length of the contig at which the cumulative contig length first reaches 50% of the total assembly. A high N50 means the assembly is contiguous; a low N50 means it is fragmented. **BUSCO** measures gene-content completeness against a curated set of single-copy orthologs; a well-assembled bacterial genome will hit >= 99% of the `bacteria_odb10` set. **Bandage** is a GUI for viewing the assembly graph (the GFA file Flye and Canu emit alongside the contigs); a clean assembly looks like a small number of linear or circular paths, a problematic assembly looks like a tangle.
- **Read simulation with badread** (Wick 2019, *Journal of Open Source Software* 4:1316; <https://github.com/rrwick/Badread>). Long-read assembly is hard to teach without a long-read dataset, and real ONT / HiFi data is large (10-100 GB per run). `badread` simulates synthetic ONT-style reads from a known reference FASTA with tunable error rate, read length distribution, and chimera fraction. Week 10 uses `badread simulate` to produce a small (~50x coverage on a 1 Mb genome) test dataset that runs through Flye + Medaka in under 10 minutes.
- **Reproducibility hygiene.** Pin tool versions (Flye 2.9.5, Canu 2.2, Hifiasm 0.19.9, Medaka 1.12.0, BUSCO 5.7.1, badread 0.4.1). Pin the Flye input mode (`--nano-hq` for modern ONT; `--nano-raw` for old ONT; `--pacbio-hifi` for HiFi; `--pacbio-raw` for legacy PacBio CLR). Pin the Medaka model (`r1041_e82_400bps_sup_v4.3.0` for Dorado SUP-basecalled R10.4.1 reads, for example). Pin the BUSCO lineage dataset (`bacteria_odb10` and the exact dataset download date). Pin the seed (`--seed 42` where the tool accepts it; record the system random state otherwise). Pin the read filter threshold (we use "drop reads shorter than 1,000 bp" throughout). Without these, two runs on the same FASTQ can produce different assemblies and the difference is impossible to debug.
- **Standard failure modes.** Undercollapsed repeats (assembler treats N copies of a tandem repeat as N distinct contigs when they are tandem; usually fixable with longer reads). Overcollapsed repeats (assembler treats K copies as 1; inflates N50, deflates total assembly length; harder to fix). Chimeric contigs (two distant regions joined by a low-confidence overlap; often visible as a bubble in Bandage). Basecaller drift (Dorado v0.5.0 reads do not basecall identically to v0.7.0 reads on the same POD5; pin the version). Polish model mismatch (Medaka model `r941_min_sup_g507` applied to R10.4.1 reads produces worse results than no polish; the model must match the basecaller). The mini-project write-up names which of these its assembly could plausibly suffer from.

## A note on tone

C10 is written in **lab-notebook voice**. We pin versions ("Flye 2.9.5", "Canu 2.2", "Hifiasm 0.19.9", "Medaka 1.12.0", "BUSCO 5.7.1", "badread 0.4.1", "Dorado 0.7.2"). We cite tools by their paper ("Flye, Kolmogorov et al. 2019, *Nat Biotechnol* 37:540"; "Canu, Koren et al. 2017, *Genome Res* 27:722"; "Hifiasm, Cheng et al. 2021, *Nat Methods* 18:170"; "BUSCO, Manni et al. 2021, *Mol Biol Evol* 38:4647"). We say "Flye assembled the 50x simulated nanopore reads from a 1.04 Mb reference into 3 contigs totaling 1.038 Mb in 47 seconds; the N50 is 521,883 bp; after one round of Medaka polishing with the `r1041_e82_400bps_sup_v4.3.0` model, BUSCO reports 99.2% complete single-copy from the `bacteria_odb10` set" rather than "Flye produced an assembly." A reproducible pipeline reports the version of every tool, the input mode, the model name, the seed, and the QC numbers. The numbers are the work.

## A note on the data size

A simulated 50x ONT-style read set from a 1 Mb reference is ~50 MB in FASTQ. A real ONT R10.4 run on a small bacterial genome (say *E. coli*, 4.6 Mb) produces ~100 GB of POD5 signal plus ~10-20 GB of basecalled FASTQ. A real PacBio HiFi run on a small genome produces ~5-10 GB of FASTQ. The Week 10 didactic dataset is the simulated 50x on a 1 Mb reference; this runs through `badread` + `flye` + `medaka` in under 10 minutes on a 16 GB laptop and produces ~200 MB of intermediate files. The mini-project ramps the genome size up to ~5 Mb, which runs in 30-60 minutes end-to-end.

Commit the input reference (small), the simulation parameters, the simulated FASTQ (gzipped), the Flye assembly FASTA, the Flye GFA, the polished assembly FASTA, the BUSCO summary, the rendered Bandage screenshot, and the `run-info.json`. Gitignore the Flye intermediate directory (`flye_out/00-assembly/`, `flye_out/10-consensus/`, etc.; large, reproducible from the inputs and the seed), the Medaka intermediate BAMs, and any uncompressed FAST5 / POD5 archives.

## Stretch goals

If you finish early and want to push further, try any of the following:

- Read the Flye paper (Kolmogorov et al. 2019) end to end at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6699608/>. The repeat-graph construction in §2 is the technical heart of the method and explains why Flye handles repetitive bacterial chromosomes better than older OLC assemblers.
- Read the HiFi paper (Wenger et al. 2019) at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6776680/>. The CCS protocol section explains why HiFi reads are different from raw PacBio CLR reads and why HiFi-specialized assemblers (Hifiasm) make different design choices from OLC assemblers (Flye, Canu).
- Add a second polish round with **Pilon** (Walker et al. 2014, *PLoS ONE* 9:e112963; free at <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4237348/>) using a paired-end Illumina read set as a complementary error correction. This is the conventional "long-read assembly + short-read polish" hybrid approach.
- Try a different read length distribution in `badread`: simulate ultra-long reads (N50 ~100 kb) and see whether the assembly contiguity (N50 of the output) tracks the read N50. The rule of thumb is "assembly N50 is roughly bounded by the read N50 for repetitive regions wider than the read length."
- Compute a per-base assembly quality score with `minimap2 + samtools` (Li 2018, *Bioinformatics* 34:3094) by aligning the polished assembly back to the original reference and tallying matches / mismatches / indels. Report **QV** (the Phred-scaled error rate): QV30 means one error per 1,000 bp; QV40 means one per 10,000; HiFi assemblies typically hit QV45-50.
- Reproduce a small published long-read assembly end-to-end. The *E. coli* K-12 MG1655 ONT R10.4 dataset on the EBI ENA (run accession `SRR23984905` or similar) is a common target. Your assembly should agree with the GenBank reference (`U00096.3`) on >= 99.9% of bases after one round of Medaka polishing.
